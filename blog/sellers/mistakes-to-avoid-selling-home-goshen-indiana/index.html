What Mistakes Should I Avoid When Selling My Home in Goshen, Indiana?

Here’s the pattern worth knowing before anything else: nearly every expensive mistake a Goshen seller can make happens before the sign goes in the yard. By the time a listing is struggling, the errors are already weeks old — which is bad news for the seller in that position and very good news for the one still planning, because every one of these mistakes is avoidable with a little foresight.

The short answer: the mistakes that cost Goshen sellers the most are overpricing at launch, skipping preparation, settling for weak photos, ignoring what the market data is saying, and choosing an agent based on the most flattering number rather than the most honest one. All five are decisions made — or dodged — before listing day.

Mistake one: pricing on hope

Overpricing is the most expensive mistake in real estate, and it never feels like one at the time — it feels prudent, “leaving room to negotiate.” What actually happens: buyers comparing in real time tour the better-priced competition, the listing stales, reductions follow, and the eventual sale price lands below what an honest launch would likely have brought. The first two weeks of a listing are its strongest; a hopeful price spends them convincing buyers to look elsewhere.

Mistake two: skipping the preparation

“Buyers can see past the clutter” is a comforting theory that showings disprove daily. Buyers price what they see — and what they see in an unprepared home is work. The fix costs remarkably little: cleaning, decluttering, paint, lighting, curb appeal. Skipping a few hundred dollars of preparation routinely costs thousands in the offer, which makes it one of the worst trades available to a seller.

Mistake three: weak photos in a phone-first market

Your home’s first showing happens on a screen, in seconds. Dim, tilted, phone-camera photos don’t just undersell the home — they filter out buyers who would have loved it in person and never came. Professional photography, video, and drone footage aren’t luxuries in this market; they’re the price of being seen at your home’s actual value.

Mistake four: arguing with the data

Once listed, the market talks — through showing counts, buyer feedback, and online activity. Sellers who treat that data as noise, waiting months for “the right buyer” while the evidence points to price or presentation, pay for the delay twice: in carrying costs and in the weaker position a stale listing negotiates from. Lisa’s twice-weekly reports exist precisely so that conversation happens early, on numbers, while adjusting still works.

Mistake five: choosing the flattering number

The quietest mistake happens when picking an agent. Interview three, and the temptation is to hire whoever names the highest price — but a listing price isn’t an offer, and an agent willing to buy your signature with an inflated number has told you something about what the next three months of advice will sound like. Choose the agent whose number comes with reasoning you can inspect: the comparables, the adjustments, the strategy. Truth over pressure ages a lot better than flattery.

A story about the second time around

A couple selling their Goshen home had done this once before, years earlier in another town — and done it the hard way. That sale started with the highest number any agent would say out loud, sat through a summer of silence, and closed after two price cuts at a figure they still winced about. This time they came in asking better questions, and the plan was built to answer them: a launch price they could see the comparables behind, a consult-guided fortnight of preparation, professional photos, and full marketing in two languages. The home drew steady showings from the first weekend and sold on strong terms within the month. Same couple, similar house — entirely different outcome, and the only variable that changed was the strategy.

Frequently Asked Questions

What’s the single most expensive selling mistake?

Overpricing at launch — it burns the listing’s strongest weeks convincing buyers to tour the competition, and it usually ends in a lower final price than honest pricing would have produced.

Can I fix these mistakes after listing?

Mostly, yes — a decisive price correction, better photos, and a genuine relaunch can revive a stuck listing. But prevention is cheaper than repair, in both money and time.

How do I know an agent’s suggested price is honest?

Ask to see the reasoning: the recent comparable sales, the adjustments, and the competition. An honest number survives that inspection; a flattering one changes the subject.

Final thoughts

The good news buried in every selling mistake is that it’s a decision — and decisions can be made well. With honest pricing, disciplined preparation, and marketing at full strength, the expensive errors simply never get the chance to happen.
