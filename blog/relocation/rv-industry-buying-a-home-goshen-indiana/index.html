Working in the RV Industry and Buying a Home in Goshen, Indiana

If you're moving to Goshen for a job in the RV industry, you're in the majority. The corridor running through Goshen, Elkhart, and Middlebury produces the great majority of North America's recreational vehicles, and it is the single most common reason people relocate here.

It also changes how you should approach buying a home — in three specific ways that most relocation guides won't tell you, because most relocation guides aren't written by someone who has sat across the table from people in exactly this position.

First: find out which plant, not which company

This sounds obvious. It is routinely the thing people get wrong.

Thor Industries is headquartered in Elkhart. Forest River, Keystone RV, Dutchmen Manufacturing, Jayco in Middlebury, and Lippert Components all operate multiple facilities scattered across the area. The supply chain runs wider still — Benteler builds automotive components, and custom body builders like Supreme and Showhauler Trucks work here too.

"I got a job at Forest River" tells you almost nothing about where you should live. The company name is not the address. Get the specific facility, and if you can, drive the route at the hour you'd actually drive it. The difference between a ten-minute commute and a thirty-five-minute one will shape your daily life more than any feature of the house you're touring — and in this industry, where shifts often start early, that gap gets multiplied by however many years you're there.

Goshen puts you close to plants on the south end of the corridor. Elkhart is better positioned for the north side and gets you nearer the Toll Road. Neither is automatically right. It depends entirely on where you're reporting.

Second: production pay is documented differently

Many plants in this industry pay on a production basis rather than a straight hourly wage — you build the day's units and go home. That structure is normal here and it's a big part of why the work attracts people. It also means take-home pay varies from week to week, and the total hours vary too.

That matters when you go to buy a house, not because it's a problem, but because it's documented differently than a salary. Lenders generally want to see a history of that income before they'll count it, and the specifics of how much history and what form it takes depend on the loan program and the lender.

The practical instruction: start the financing conversation early, and be upfront about how you're paid. Someone new to a production job may need more documentation than they expect, and the time to find that out is before you're competing for a house, not while you're under contract on one. Ask the lender directly how they'll treat your pay structure.

Third: the industry has a cycle, and this area feels it

Here's the part nobody puts in a brochure.

RVs are a discretionary purchase. When the economy is strong and credit is loose, people buy them; when it isn't, they don't — and they don't with remarkable speed. That makes RV manufacturing one of the more cyclical industries in the country, and because so much of it is concentrated in this one corridor, the whole area moves with it.

The clearest illustration is the 2008–2009 downturn, when RV demand collapsed and unemployment across this area rose to among the highest levels in the nation. The recovery since has been substantial — the industry came back strong and the area came back with it. But anyone buying a house here on RV income should understand the shape of the thing they're standing on.

Shorter cycles are part of ordinary life too. Plants take shutdown weeks. Slow stretches happen. Long-tenured people in this industry build their household budgets around that reality rather than around a good month.

What that actually means for how much house to buy

Not "buy less house." Buy with a buffer.

The mistake Lisa sees most often isn't people buying the wrong house. It's people buying at the absolute ceiling of what they were approved for, during a strong stretch, with nothing behind the payment. The house is fine. The math is fine on the day of closing. Then a slow quarter arrives and there's no slack anywhere in the budget.

A payment you can carry through a soft patch without dread is worth considerably more than an extra bedroom. That's not caution for its own sake — it's what lets you stay in the house.

The other piece: know your own timeline. Someone here for a specific five-year stretch is buying differently than someone putting down roots. Both are legitimate. They just aren't the same purchase, and the difference should show up in what you buy and how long you plan to hold it. That's the core of how Lisa works — The Next Chapter Method™ starts with the season of life you're in and lets the house serve it.

And what it means for resale

A concentrated local economy cuts both ways on the way out, too. When the industry is strong, this market has energy. When it softens, that shows up in how long homes sit.

You can't control the cycle. You can control two things: not being forced to sell at the wrong moment, which is what the buffer is actually for, and buying something with broad appeal rather than a niche property that needs exactly the right buyer. One thing worth knowing

A buyer relocating for a supplier position once came to Lisa fully pre-approved and wanting to spend every dollar of it. He'd been at the new job about four months, the money was good, and he had a number in his head.

Lisa asked him one question: what happens to this payment during a shutdown week?

He didn't have an answer, because nobody had told him shutdown weeks were a thing. They looked at houses about fifteen percent below his ceiling instead. Two years later he told her that decision was the reason he'd never once been anxious about the mortgage — and he'd finished the basement with what he hadn't spent.

That's not a story about being conservative. It's a story about buying with accurate information about the industry you're joining, which is a different thing entirely, and it's the part a relocating buyer usually can't get from anywhere but a local.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is it hard to buy a home with RV production income?

Not inherently — plenty of people here do it. It's documented differently than salaried income, so start the lender conversation early and be direct about how you're paid.

Should I live in Goshen or Elkhart if I work in the RV industry?

It depends entirely on which facility you report to, not which company employs you. Get the specific address and drive the commute before you decide. More on the alternative on the Elkhart community page.

How stable is RV industry employment in this area?

The industry is cyclical — RVs are a discretionary purchase, and demand moves with the broader economy. That's not a reason to avoid buying here. It's a reason to buy with room in the budget.

Thinking about a move for an RV industry job?

Lisa works with relocating buyers in this exact situation regularly — including plenty who are still living somewhere else while they figure it out. She's glad to talk through commutes, plant locations, neighborhoods, and what a realistic budget looks like given how you're paid.
