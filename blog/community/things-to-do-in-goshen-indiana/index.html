Things to Do in Goshen, Indiana

Goshen surprises people. It's a city of about 34,000 — the Maple City, established 1831 — which usually predicts a short list. The honest reality is that the list here is longer than it should be for the population, mostly because the downtown never got hollowed out the way a lot of Midwestern downtowns did. In 2017 the Indiana Chamber of Commerce named Goshen the state's Community of the Year, and the reasons are mostly visible on foot.

Here's what's actually worth doing, organized roughly by how much time you have.

Downtown, if you have an afternoon

Goshen's Historic District was established in 1983 and runs about 45 city blocks. The storefronts along Main Street date to the late 1800s and early 1900s and are still in commercial use — not preserved as a museum, just still working. Parking downtown is free, and the city publishes an interactive map of the lots.

Independent retail anchors it:

  • The Nut Shoppe — downtown more than 25 years, hand-dipped chocolates, homemade caramels, turtles
  • Olympia Candy Kitchen — an old-fashioned candy store attached to one of the more intact vintage diner counters left in the region; the breakfast is the local secret
  • Fables Books — used books, the kind of store you lose an hour in
  • Found and Ten Thousand Villages — handcrafted goods sourced internationally; Found also carries vintage lighting and antiques
  • The Soapy Gnome — makes its soaps on site, and you can usually watch
  • Woldruff's Footwear, Jules Boutique, and Twice as Nice — shoes, new clothing, and high-end consignment
  • Maple City Market — a locally owned grocery co-op
  • Flowers by Phoebe — arrangements from blooms grown on their own small urban farm
  • Several thrift stores, including The Depot, which is genuinely large

Downtown also operates as a Designated Outdoor Refreshment Area (DORA), which allows open containers from participating businesses inside a defined boundary. On a summer evening that changes the whole feel of the district.

Six Goshen properties are on the National Register of Historic Places: the Goshen Historic District itself, the Elkhart County Courthouse at its center, the Fort Wayne Street Bridge, the Goshen Carnegie Public Library, the William N. Violett House, and the Violett-Martin House and Gardens. An hour spent finding them is a better architectural tour than most cities this size can offer.

The Goshen Public Library and the Goshen Historical Society museum — housed in one of the city's original store buildings, wheelchair accessible, about an hour to see — are both downtown and both free. While you're there, look for the small police booth built during the John Dillinger era, back when a bank in a town this size had reason to be nervous.

The Old Bag Factory

North of downtown, a former industrial building has been converted into working studios and shops. Swartzendruber Hardwood Creations and John Mishler Sculpture Studio are the best-known tenants, alongside makers producing quilts, ironware, stained glass, soaps, and baked goods.

The distinction that matters: these are working studios, not a gift shop with a theme. You can usually watch someone building the thing you're about to buy, which is a different experience than retail.

Goshen Theater

The Goshen Theater at 216 S. Main Street opened in 1905 as the Jefferson Theatre, burned down a year later, was rebuilt in 1907, spent decades as a movie house, closed in the 1980s, and served as a church for nearly thirty years before a nonprofit bought it in 2014 and restored it.

It's now a 600-seat venue programming live music, theater, dance, film, and community events. Worth knowing: the theater offers assistive listening devices, sensory backpacks with noise-cancelling headphones and fidget tools, and a range of seating options — all free to check out at the box office.

New World Arts also produces downtown, with a range that runs from musicals to drama.

Where to eat

Goshen punches considerably above its weight here, and two places have national footprints.

Venturi makes Neapolitan pizza in a converted downtown space — readers of Esquire once named it among the most memorable pizzas in the country. South Side Soda Shop has been visited by Guy Fieri for Food Network's Diners, Drive-Ins and Dives; the pies are the reason, though the sandwiches hold up.

Beyond those:

  • Goshen Brewing Company — house beer, a serious kitchen, and live music
  • Which Craft Brewery — the other local option
  • Maple Indian — downtown, with an affordable buffet at lunch
  • Miso Japan — sushi and Japanese; expect a wait on weekends
  • Noodlelicious — pho and Vietnamese
  • Anna's Bread — house-made breads and pastries next to the Farmers Market, plus full meals
  • A deep bench of Mexican food, which reflects the city honestly: Universal Tamal, Los Primos Mexican Grill, and Tacos El Portal, a permanent taco truck parked north of downtown on Pike Street
  • Jojo's Pretzels and Shirley's Popcorn for the in-between hours

Coffee and dessert

The Electric Brew on East Washington Street is the one locals name first — an open, noisy, everyone-goes-there workspace. Embassy Coffee Co. roasts and pours single-origin coffee with juices and smoothies alongside.

For dessert, The Chief has been ranked among the better ice cream shops in the country and is the summer default. Ask about blue moon — it's a Midwest thing, and it's better than it sounds. Honey's rotates frozen yogurt flavors constantly.

Outdoors

The Pumpkinvine Nature Trail is the headline — a paved rail-trail running northeast out of Goshen, built on a former rail corridor, with connecting routes linking Goshen, Elkhart, and Shipshewana.

Inside the city, Fidler Pond Park has water, paths, and seasonal pedal boat, kayak, and canoe rentals. Abshire Park sits along the Elkhart River, and Water Tower Park, Hay Park, and Mullet Park fill in elsewhere. There's a skatepark, plus two dog parks — Robert L. Nelson and Sprocket.

The Millrace, Goshen's historic canal, has a path alongside it that feeds into the Maple City Greenway, the city's connected trail network. Ox Bow Park, a county park a short drive north along the river, adds trails and a disc golf course.

Seasonally, Kercher's Sunrise Orchard draws people out for apples, and Stutsman Ballooning operates hot air balloon flights over the countryside.

Ordinary evenings

Not everything needs to be an occasion. Linway Plaza on Lincoln Avenue, just west of downtown, holds a ten-screen cinema with stadium seating and a cluster of restaurants — American, Chinese, Italian, Mexican. There's laser tag and an arcade in town. GoDance Studio runs West Coast swing, Latin, and ballroom nights with a lesson beforehand. Spacious Heart Yoga covers the other end of the evening.

The Goshen Farmers Market runs on a regular weekly schedule and is a fixture rather than a novelty. Check the market's own calendar for current days and hours.

A little local trivia

Goshen is pronounced GOH-shen, and it takes its name from the biblical Land of Goshen. The 2005 film Lonesome Jim — directed by Steve Buscemi, starring Liv Tyler and Casey Affleck — was written by a former Goshen resident and shot here. The band Lotus formed at Goshen College. Film director Howard Hawks, NFL quarterback Rick Mirer, and CNN anchor Kate Bolduan all came from here.

Something genuinely unusual

Janus Motorcycles hand-builds small-displacement motorcycles in Goshen and ships them across the country. A boutique motorcycle manufacturer is not what most people expect to find in a city this size, and it says something accurate about the place — Goshen has a persistent streak of people making physical things well, from the studios at the Old Bag Factory to the RV plants down the road.

Day trips from Goshen

Almost everything below is inside half an hour.

Elkhart, about ten miles north, has Wellfield Botanic Gardens, the RV/MH Hall of Fame and Museum, Ruthmere (a Beaux-Arts mansion museum), and Linton's Enchanted Gardens. The county's Heritage Trail driving tour connects the towns, and the Quilt Gardens — large quilt-pattern flower plantings installed across the county each summer — are genuinely unusual and free to see.

Shipshewana, about fifteen miles east, is known for its flea market, specialty shops, the Blue Gate Theatre, and Menno-Hof, a visitor center interpreting Mennonite and Amish history. Das Dutchman Essenhaus in Middlebury is one of Indiana's larger restaurants and a long-standing regional draw. Guided buggy and farm tours operate throughout the surrounding countryside, and a country drive here routinely turns up small home businesses selling baked goods, quilts, and furniture.

Further out: South Bend, about 23 miles northwest, has the University of Notre Dame, the Studebaker National Museum, and the Potawatomi Zoo. Chicago is roughly two and a half hours by car — worth remembering that Goshen is on Eastern time and Chicago is on Central.

One suggestion, from an agent

If you're evaluating Goshen rather than just visiting it, come on a first Friday of the month. It's the one evening where the city is unmistakably itself — the whole downtown is out, the storefronts are open late, and you get an honest read in three hours that would otherwise take three visits.

More than one buyer relocating from a larger metro has arrived expecting an uneventful Friday night and left having changed their mind about the whole town. What's actually happening in that moment isn't the event — it's that they're seeing the place function, which is impossible to get from listing photos.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Goshen, Indiana best known for?

Its historic downtown business district, the Old Bag Factory, the Pumpkinvine Nature Trail, and its place in the RV manufacturing corridor.

Is there much to do in Goshen year-round?

Yes — First Fridays runs every month regardless of season, the Goshen Theater programs through the winter, and the Elkhart County 4-H Fair anchors the summer. See the full year-round events calendar.

How far is Goshen from Chicago?

About two and a half hours by car via the Indiana Toll Road. Goshen observes Eastern time and Chicago is on Central.
