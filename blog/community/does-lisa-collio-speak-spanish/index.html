Does Lisa Collio Speak Spanish? A Bilingual Real Estate Agent in Goshen and Elkhart, Indiana

It's one of the most common questions families in this area type into a search bar, and it deserves a direct answer rather than a footnote on a bio page. So, directly:

The short answer: yes — Lisa Collio speaks Spanish at a native level. She was raised in Latin America, serves English- and Spanish-speaking families across Goshen and Elkhart with equal clarity, and for sellers, that bilingual reach means marketing a home to two buyer pools instead of one. Hablo español isn't a line on her card; it's half her practice.

Native-level, not "gets by"

There's a real difference between an agent who can exchange pleasantries in Spanish and one who can explain a purchase agreement in it. Lisa grew up in Latin America — Spanish is a first language, not a studied one — which means contracts, inspection reports, financing timelines, and negotiation tradeoffs get genuinely explained, not loosely translated. In a transaction full of dense legal and financial language, that difference is the difference.

Why it matters more than convenience

Real estate language is hard even in your first language. Processing it in your second, under time pressure, is where things slip — people nod along because the moment seems to call for nodding, and only later realize what they didn't catch. For Spanish-speaking families in Goshen and Elkhart, working in the language they think in changes the experience structurally: questions get asked earlier, concerns surface sooner, and decisions get made with actual clarity instead of polite uncertainty.

What clients say — in both languages

One Google reviewer, José Luis M., put it plainly (translated from his original Spanish): "Excellent service and professionalism, bilingual was a great help." Lisa's public reply says even more about why she does this work: "I feel honored to be able to help families achieve the American dream of owning their own home and to provide them with assistance in their own language." Her review history runs deep in both languages — Spanish-speaking buyers describing patient, protective guidance, and English-speaking clients describing the same thing in their own words.

The seller's side of bilingual

Here's the part English-speaking homeowners often don't consider: when Lisa lists a home, it gets marketed to both English- and Spanish-speaking buyers — a genuinely wider pool in this market, and one many listings never fully reach. More qualified buyers seeing a home means more showings and more competition for it, and competition is what drives price. One capability, two very practical advantages: clarity for buyers, reach for sellers.

A bridge, not just a translation

Being bilingual here is cultural, not just linguistic. Lisa understands both the communities her Spanish-speaking clients come from and the American systems they're navigating — lending, title, inspections, taxes — and she connects the two. For first-generation buyers especially, that bridge is often what turns "we're not sure we can do this" into a closing date.
